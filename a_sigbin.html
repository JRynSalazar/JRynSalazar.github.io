Sigbin: The Inverted Beast

Backwards-walking horror of the night

Origin

The Sigbin is a creature feared in Visayan and Mindanaoan folklore, said to emerge from the shadows during Holy Week. It resembles a hornless goat with dark skin, large ears it can clap like hands, and a body that moves backward — walking in a chilling, reverse motion.

This mythical creature is believed to suck blood from its victims through their shadows and is said to feed on the hearts of children.

Cultural Significance

Some stories link Sigbin to wealthy families who keep one hidden in clay jars or enchanted cages, using its powers for protection or malevolent intent. In some accounts, aswang or other shapeshifters are said to command them.

A Sigbin’s presence is often associated with sudden illness, strange animal footprints, or livestock behaving erratically at night.

Modern Interpretation

In modern urban tales, Sigbin is often cast as a terrifying cryptid-like entity. It has appeared in several horror anthologies and paranormal documentaries in the Philippines.

Some researchers link Sigbin legends to sightings of actual rare animals, or interpret the stories as metaphors for rural fears and colonial trauma.
